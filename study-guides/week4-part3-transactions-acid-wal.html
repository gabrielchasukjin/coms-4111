Transactions, ACID & Write-Ahead Logging

How databases guarantee your data survives crashes and stays consistent — with real-world examples and hands-on simulations

1. Why Should You Care?

Before we talk about any theory, let's talk about real disasters that happened because databases didn't handle this correctly.

Knight Capital Group — August 1, 2012

A software bug caused Knight Capital's trading system to execute millions of unintended stock trades in 45 minutes. The system was buying high and selling low on 154 different stocks. By the time anyone pulled the plug, the firm had lost $440 million — effectively destroying the company overnight.

The root cause? A deployment error left old code running alongside new code, and the system had no transactional safeguards to ensure trades were executed as all-or-nothing units. Partial, inconsistent state crept in, and chaos followed.

Your Everyday Life

You Venmo your friend $50 for dinner. Your balance drops by $50, but right at that moment Venmo's server crashes. When it restarts: did your friend get the $50? Did your balance go back up? Or is the money just... gone?

This is exactly the problem transactions solve. Without them, every app that touches money, medical records, flight bookings, or grades is a ticking time bomb.

Transactions are not some abstract database concept. They are the reason your bank account doesn't randomly lose money, your flight reservation doesn't double-book your seat, and your medical records don't get half-updated with someone else's data.

2. Two Kinds of Database Work

Not all database usage is the same. When a Visa card is swiped at a grocery store, the system needs to check your balance, authorize the charge, and update the records — all in milliseconds, while thousands of other people are swiping their cards at the exact same moment.

Compare that to a data analyst at Netflix who runs a query like "What was our subscriber growth by country last quarter?" This query might scan billions of rows and take minutes, but nobody is waiting with their credit card out.

OLTP — Online Transaction Processing

Think: the cash register, the ATM, the "Buy Now" button.

  • Many short, fast operations
  • Reads AND writes
  • Millisecond latency — users are waiting
  • Thousands of users at once

Real examples: Visa processes 65,000 transactions per second. Uber handles millions of ride requests daily. Every "Add to Cart" on Amazon is an OLTP transaction.

OLAP — Online Analytical Processing

Think: the quarterly business report, the recommendation model.

  • Few complex queries
  • Mostly reads (scanning huge datasets)
  • Seconds to hours — nobody staring at a spinner
  • A handful of analysts

Real examples: "Which products have declining margins?" "What content should we recommend to cat video watchers?" Training ML models on user behavior data.

This entire lecture is about the OLTP world — where thousands of users are simultaneously reading and writing, any operation could crash mid-flight, and correctness is non-negotiable.

A bank needs to apply 6% interest to 10 million savings accounts at the end of the month. Is this OLTP or OLAP?

3. What Is a Transaction?

Transaction: A sequence of one or more database operations that is treated as a single, indivisible unit of work. Either ALL of the operations succeed and become permanent, or NONE of them do.

Let's make this viscerally concrete. Forget databases for a second and think about your real life.

The Airline Booking

You're booking a round-trip flight from New York to Tokyo. The booking involves three steps:

  1. Reserve seat on NYC → Tokyo flight
  2. Reserve seat on Tokyo → NYC return flight
  3. Charge your credit card $1,200

What if the system completes step 1 and 3, but step 2 fails because the return flight is full? You've been charged $1,200, you have a one-way ticket to Tokyo, and no way home.

A transaction ensures: either all three steps succeed (you get both flights and you're charged), or none of them happen (no flights, no charge). There is no in-between.

Transaction Syntax in SQL

START TRANSACTION;
  UPDATE accounts SET balance = balance - 50 WHERE id = 'alice';
  UPDATE accounts SET balance = balance + 50 WHERE id = 'bob';
COMMIT; — make both changes permanent
(or ROLLBACK; — discard everything, as if nothing happened)

The Bank Interest Problem

A bank needs to apply 6% annual interest to every savings account. There are 1,000 accounts. The transaction reads each balance, computes new_balance = old_balance × 1.06, and writes it back.

The Crash Problem

The system crashes after updating 500 of the 1,000 accounts.

  • 500 customers got their 6% interest — their balances are updated
  • 500 customers did NOT get interest — their balances are unchanged

This is a lawsuit waiting to happen. Some customers got free money, others got shortchanged, and the bank's books don't balance.

Think of a transaction like a light switch. It's either ON or OFF. There is no "halfway flipped" state. If something interrupts you mid-flip, the switch must end up in one of the two valid positions — never stuck in between.

4. The ACID Properties

ACID is the set of four guarantees that every serious transactional database provides. Each letter addresses a different way things can go wrong.

A — Atomicity

"All or nothing."

Either every operation succeeds, or none take effect. If the bank interest crashes after 500 accounts, atomicity rolls back all 500. Zero accounts get interest, not half.

Real life: When you withdraw cash from an ATM, either the machine dispenses cash AND your balance decreases, or neither happens. You never get charged without receiving cash.

C — Consistency

"Valid state to valid state."

The database starts consistent and ends consistent. All constraints hold. Money doesn't appear from nowhere or vanish.

Real life: In a bank transfer, total money across all accounts is the same before and after. Alice had $100, Bob had $200 (total $300) — after any transfer the total is still $300.

I — Isolation

"No interference."

Concurrent transactions don't see each other's half-finished work. Each executes as if it were the only one running.

Real life: Two bank tellers processing transfers simultaneously can't accidentally double-spend the same $100. Each teller sees a consistent view.

D — Durability

"Committed = permanent."

Once you see "Transaction committed," the changes survive anything — power outage, server crash, earthquake.

Real life: Once the ATM says "Withdrawal complete" and you have cash in hand, your bank can't later say "actually, that didn't happen."

Notice something?

Two of the four — Atomicity and Durability — are both enforced by the same mechanism: Write-Ahead Logging (WAL). This is not a coincidence. WAL is the workhorse that makes crash recovery possible.

Isolation is enforced by locking and serializability (next lecture). Consistency is enforced by application logic and constraints.

5. Physical Logging — The Building Block

Before we get to WAL, we need the tool it uses: physical logging. The idea is beautifully simple.

Physical Logging: Before modifying any data on disk, first write a log record describing exactly what the change is. This record captures enough information to both undo and redo the change.

The Home Renovation Analogy

You're remodeling your kitchen. Before you rip out the old countertop, you take a photo of how it looks (OldValue). Then a photo of the design plan for the new one (NewValue). You write both in a notebook with the room name and exact location.

If the contractor ghosts you mid-renovation, you use the "before" photo to restore the original (UNDO). If the renovation was completed but someone questions it, you use the "after" photo to verify or redo it (REDO). That notebook is the log.

What a Log Record Looks Like

[TransactionID, PageID, Offset, OldValue, NewValue]

  • TransactionID: Which transaction made this change (e.g., "T1")
  • PageID: Which page of the database file was modified
  • Offset: Where on that page the change happened
  • OldValue: What was there before — needed to UNDO
  • NewValue: What we changed it to — needed to REDO

For the bank example, updating account #1 from $1,000 to $1,060 creates:

[T1, Page5, Offset12, $1000, $1060]

UNDO — Roll Back

Restore the OldValue. Used when a transaction crashed before committing. Its partial changes must be erased.

"The renovation was never finished. Put the old countertop back."

REDO — Replay Forward

Apply the NewValue. Used when a transaction committed but data pages might not be on disk.

"The renovation was approved and paid for. Make sure the new countertop is installed."

A log record says [T7, Page12, Offset5, "John", "Jonathan"]. T7 crashed before committing. What does recovery do?

6. Write-Ahead Logging (WAL) — The Protocol

We know what log records look like and what UNDO/REDO do. The crucial question is: when do we write these records to disk? Get the ordering wrong, and the database cannot recover.

Write-Ahead Logging (WAL) dictates the order: the log is always written ahead of (before) the data. Log first, data second. Always.

The Two Rules

1 The Durability Rule

Before acknowledging a COMMIT to the user, ALL of that transaction's log records must be flushed to disk.

Why? If we tell you "transfer complete!" but the log isn't on disk yet, a crash loses it. We can't REDO your transfer. Your money is gone. Durability violated.

2 The Atomicity Rule

Before writing a modified data page to disk, ALL log records for that page must be flushed first.

Why? If modified data gets to disk before the log, a crash leaves corrupted data with no way to undo it. Atomicity violated.

What Happens If You Break These Rules?

Wrong Approach #1: Write data first, then log

You update account balances on disk, then plan to write log records. Crash happens between the two.

Result: Partially modified data on disk, no log to undo it. Database permanently corrupted. Atomicity destroyed.

Wrong Approach #2: Acknowledge COMMIT before log is done

You tell the user "Transfer complete!" but haven't finished writing all log records. Crash happens.

Result: COMMIT record exists but some change records are missing. Can't fully redo the transfer. Durability destroyed.

Think of the WAL log as a black box flight recorder. It must always be recording before the plane does anything. If the plane crashes, you read the recorder to reconstruct what happened. If the recorder wasn't running, you have wreckage with no explanation.

Crash Recovery — The Payoff

COMMIT record found?

YES → REDO. Replay all changes using NewValues. Committed work is restored even if data pages weren't on disk yet. Durability guaranteed.

No COMMIT record?

NO → UNDO. Reverse all changes using OldValues. Partial work completely erased. Atomicity guaranteed.

Crash Scenario Simulator

The bank is applying interest to 1,000 accounts. Choose when the crash happens.

Scenario A: Crash after 500 updates, before COMMIT

Log has 500 change records but no COMMIT. 500 data pages modified in memory.

Scenario B: Crash after COMMIT in log, before data pages flushed

Log has all 1,000 records AND the COMMIT. But disk data pages are stale.

Scenario C: Crash after everything written to disk

Log has COMMIT, all data pages flushed. Then crash.

A transaction updated 3 rows, COMMIT was written and flushed to the log, then the server crashed before data pages were written. After recovery, what state are the 3 rows in?

7. Why WAL Is Practically Free

You might be thinking: "All these log records sound expensive." They're not, and the reason is physics.

Random Writes

Writing data pages to scattered disk locations. The disk head must seek to each spot.

1 million pages: ~100,000 seconds (over a day)

Sequential Writes

Writing data to consecutive locations. No seeking.

1 million pages: ~1 second

That's 100,000x faster. Five orders of magnitude.

The Amazon Warehouse Analogy

Random writes are like an Amazon worker who, for every package, walks to a random spot in a warehouse the size of 10 football fields, places the package, walks back, gets the next one. All day.

Sequential writes are like standing at a conveyor belt, placing packages one after another as they roll by. Same number of packages — but the worker never moves. That's WAL: append-only, always writing to the end, never seeking.

The WAL log is append-only = always sequential = essentially free in terms of performance. You get crash recovery at negligible cost.

8. The Complete Picture

The Chain of Reasoning

  1. Real-world operations (bank transfers, flight bookings) must be all-or-nothing
  2. We wrap them in transactions
  3. Transactions need ACID properties
  4. Atomicity and Durability are hardest because of crashes
  5. Physical logging records every change with OldValue/NewValue for UNDO/REDO
  6. WAL specifies: log to disk BEFORE data pages, BEFORE acknowledging COMMIT
  7. Recovery: COMMIT in log → REDO. No COMMIT → UNDO.
  8. WAL's append-only log uses sequential writes = ~100,000x faster

Real scenario: A doctor submits a prescription. The system must (1) add it to the patient record, (2) update the pharmacy queue, (3) deduct from insurance. Server crashes after step 2. With WAL, what happens?

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Transactions are all-or-nothing — like a flight booking: everything succeeds or nothing does
  • ✓ ACID protects against crashes (A, D), concurrent access (I), and data corruption (C)
  • ✓ WAL = log first, data second — two rules ensure atomicity and durability
  • ✓ Recovery is simple — COMMIT in log = REDO. No COMMIT = UNDO.
  • ✓ Sequential writes make WAL ~100,000x faster than the alternative
