Continuing from Part 7, here is the full LFU trace for 1, 2, 3, 4, 1, 2, 5, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 with cache size 4. Note: LFU tracks historical frequency counts — even after an item is evicted, its count is remembered so that if it returns, its frequency reflects its full history.

4 Hit Increment 4's count [1, 2, 3, 4] 1:3, 2:3, 3:2, 4:2
5 Miss Evict 3 or 4 (both count 2, randomly chose 4) [1, 2, 3, 5] 1:3, 2:3, 3:2, 5:2

Result: 7 misses, 5 hits out of 12 requests

Hit rate: 5/12 = 41.7% — better than both FIFO (16.7%) and LRU (33.3%)!

LFU's advantage on this sequence: items 1 and 2 are the most frequently accessed (3 times each). LFU recognizes their high frequency and protects them from eviction, even when they haven't been accessed in a while. Note that 3's count is remembered as 2 when it re-enters (its historical count of 1 from before eviction + the new access).

Important: Tie-breaking is Random

When multiple items have the same lowest frequency (e.g., items 3 and 4 both at count 1), LFU must break the tie somehow. In this example, the tie is broken randomly. Different tie-breaking strategies (e.g., FIFO among ties, or LRU among ties) can change the results.

2. LRU/LFU Combinations

LRU prioritizes recent accesses. LFU prioritizes frequent accesses. Both recency and frequency are important signals, and each works better for different workloads. This has led to many hybrid algorithms that combine both signals.

Segmented LRU

One practical hybrid approach:

  • The first time an object is inserted into the cache, place it in the middle of the LRU list (not the head/most-recent position)
  • On subsequent accesses, promote it to the top (most-recent position) as normal LRU would
  • This mostly behaves like LRU, but penalizes one-time accesses — objects that are accessed recently but only once start closer to the eviction end
Segmented LRU addresses a common LRU weakness: a single scan through many new items can evict all the popular cached items (called "cache pollution"). By inserting new items in the middle, they must prove they deserve to stay by being accessed again before reaching the protected end of the list.

3. OPT / Belady's Algorithm

Every policy we've seen (FIFO, LRU, LFU) performs better or worse depending on the workload. Is there a way to know the absolute best possible performance? Yes — Belady's algorithm (also called OPT).

OPT (Belady's Algorithm): Evict the item that will not be accessed for the longest time in the future. This is provably the optimal eviction algorithm — no other policy can achieve fewer misses.

Why It's Useful

OPT serves as a theoretical benchmark. Given a trace of requests, we can run OPT (since we know the full trace) and compare it against LRU, LFU, etc. to measure how close real policies come to optimal. This helps evaluate whether a practical policy is "good enough."

Why It's Impractical

OPT requires knowing the future — the entire sequence of future requests. In a real system, we don't know what will be requested next. So OPT cannot be implemented in practice. It exists purely as an upper bound.

Recent research has explored using OPT to train machine learning models that learn eviction policies. The idea: run OPT on historical traces, then train a model to mimic OPT's decisions using only past information (e.g., "Learning Relaxed Belady" by Song et al.).
